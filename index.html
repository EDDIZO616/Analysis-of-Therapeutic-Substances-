Unit One · Foundation

Steps Involved in Dosage Development of a New Drug

Before any drug reaches a patient, it undergoes a rigorous, multi-phase development pipeline. This unit traces the complete journey — from characterizing the raw API through preformulation studies, to designing the final dosage form, proving bioequivalence, satisfying regulatory agencies, and finally manufacturing and supplying the finished product.

Foundational Definitions

Drug
Any substance or product that is used or intended to be used to modify or explore physiological states for the benefit of the recipient.
Dosage Form
A mixture of active drug components (API) and non-drug components (excipients) processed into a final pharmaceutical preparation suitable for administration.
Dose
The quantity of medication to be administered at each time interval to produce a certain degree of pharmacological response in a patient.
Bioavailability
The rate and extent of absorption of an unchanged drug into systemic circulation. Governs how much of the administered dose reaches the site of action.
API
Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient — the biologically active substance in a drug product responsible for the intended therapeutic effect.
Excipients
Inactive pharmaceutical ingredients added for technological, biopharmaceutical, or stability reasons. Not pharmacologically active but essential for dosage form function.

Preformulation Studies of the API

Definition & Objective

Preformulation involves the application of biopharmaceutical principles to the physicochemical parameters of a drug, with the goal of designing an optimum drug delivery system. Characterization of the drug molecule is the most important step at this phase. The studies conducted depend on the type of dosage form and the nature of the drug molecule.

Peer-reviewed reference
Preformulation studies focus on physicochemical properties — drug solubility, partition coefficient, dissolution rate, polymorphic forms and stability — that affect drug performance and dosage form development. International Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences and Research (2022)
1. Solubility Determination

Intrinsic solubility (C₀): Solubility of the un-ionized form of the drug in pure water.

Saturated solubility (Cs): Maximum drug that dissolves in a solvent at a given temperature.

Cosolvents used to increase solubility — PEG 400 + 5% H₂O and Glycerin — are particularly important for IV injections where tonicity adjustment is critical.

2. pH & pKa Determination

The pKa (dissociation constant) determines the degree of ionization at any given pH. Using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:

pH = pKa + log ([A⁻] / [HA])

Ionization affects membrane permeability, absorption, distribution, renal excretion, and stability. pH also governs the choice of salt form of the drug.

3. Partition Coefficient (log P)

Measures lipophilicity — the ratio of drug concentration in octanol vs. water. Higher log P = more lipophilic.

Predicts:

  • Passive membrane permeability
  • CNS penetration potential
  • Plasma protein binding
  • Route of administration suitability
  • Risk of accumulation in fatty tissues
4. Solid-State Properties

Polymorphism: The ability of a drug to exist in more than one crystal form. Different polymorphs of the same drug can differ in:

  • Solubility and dissolution rate
  • Melting point and stability
  • Bioavailability

Amorphism: The non-crystalline, disordered solid state. Generally higher solubility than crystalline forms but lower physical stability.

5. Powder Flow Properties

Critical for tablet compression and capsule filling. Poor flow leads to weight variation and content uniformity failures.

Measured by:

  • Angle of repose
  • Carr's compressibility index
  • Hausner ratio

Glidants (e.g., colloidal SiO₂) may be added to improve flow.

6. Intrinsic Dissolution Rate

Rate at which a pure drug substance dissolves under standard conditions (fixed surface area, agitation, temperature).

Used to:

  • Predict in vivo dissolution behavior
  • Compare polymorphs or salt forms
  • Support BCS (Biopharmaceutics Classification System) classification

R&D Formulation

The pharmaceutical dosage form is a drug delivery system formed by technological processing. It determines the physical form of the final product and must reflect therapeutic intentions, route of administration, and dosing requirements.

Peer-reviewed reference
Preformulation studies provide essential information for formulation design — selection of excipients, pharmaceutical technology, and process parameters — to ensure quality, stability, and in vivo behaviour of the final dosage form. Applied Sciences, MDPI (2022)
Role of Excipients

Excipients are added for technological, biopharmaceutical, or stability reasons. Key categories include:

Diluents / Fillers Binders Lubricants Disintegrants Coatings Preservatives Stabilizers Colorants Flavourings

Formulations are developed and tested for both physical and chemical stability at accelerated temperatures per ICH guidelines before scale-up.

2a

Evaluation Tests for Dosage Forms

Weight Variation

Ensures each unit contains the correct amount of drug. Multiple tablets/capsules are weighed and checked against allowed CLSI limits.

Drug Content / Assay

Confirms drug content per unit is within the specified range — usually 90–110% of labeled amount.

Dissolution Testing

Measures how much drug is released from the dosage form into solution over time — critical predictor of bioavailability.

Stability Testing

Accelerated (40°C/75% RH) and long-term studies to predict shelf life per ICH Q1A guidelines.

Skin Irritant Test

For topical formulations — assessed using animal skin or in vitro skin models to evaluate irritancy potential.

Viscosity

For liquid and semisolid forms — affects pourability, spreadability, and drug release characteristics.

Bioequivalence (BE) Studies

Definition

Bioequivalence is a relative term denoting that the drug substance in two or more identical dosage forms reaches systemic circulation at the same relative rate and to the same relative extent. Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously.

BE studies are conducted by:

  • Pharmacokinetic method — comparing AUC (area under the curve), Cmax, and Tmax
  • Pharmacodynamic method — using a clinical endpoint or PD marker
In Vivo BE Studies

Required for:

  • Oral immediate-release products with systemic action
  • Non-oral immediate-release products
  • Modified-release products with systemic action
In Vitro BE Studies

Acceptable when:

  • Drug product slightly reformulated by original manufacturer
  • An acceptable in vitro–in vivo correlation (IVIVC) has been established
  • The in vitro dissolution rate meets regulatory standards

Unit Two · Core Methods

Analytical Techniques in the Analysis of Therapeutic & Toxic Substances

Analytical chemistry is the engine of drug development, quality control, and safety monitoring. From the earliest stages of compound discovery through to post-marketing surveillance, analytical methods provide the quantitative and qualitative data that define a drug's identity, purity, potency, and safety profile. This unit covers all major analytical platforms — titrimetric, chromatographic, spectroscopic, electrochemical, kinetic, electrophoretic, and hyphenated techniques.

Titrimetry TLC / HPTLC / HPLC / GC UV/Vis · NIRS · NMR Fluorimetry Electrochemical Capillary Electrophoresis Hyphenated Techniques
Why Analytical Techniques Matter in Drug Development

The contribution of chemistry, pharmacology, microbiology and biochemistry has set a standard in drug discovery. From the commencement of official pharmaceutical analysis, analytical assay methods were included in compendial monographs to characterize the quality of bulk drug materials. In modern practice, from drug development to post-marketing, analytical techniques play a role in:

  • Understanding physical and chemical stability of the drug
  • Influencing dosage form selection and design
  • Quantifying and identifying impurities above established thresholds
  • Assessing toxicity profiles of impurities distinct from the API
  • Assessing drug content in marketed products
  • Extensive application in pharmacokinetic studies (qualitative and quantitative analysis of drugs and metabolites)

Titrimetric Techniques

Historical note: Origin of titrimetric methods dates to the 18th century. Gay–Lussac invented the volumetric method in 1835, leading to the modern term "titration."
Principle

Titration involves the addition of one solution of known concentration (titrant / titrator) to a known volume of a solution of unknown concentration (titrand / analyte) until the reaction reaches neutralization, indicated by a color change or potentiometric endpoint.

The equivalence point is the point at which stoichiometric amounts of reactants have fully reacted — not the same as the end point (the observable signal from the indicator). The end point is designed to approximate the equivalence point as closely as possible.

Key Terms
  • Titrant (Titrator): Solution of known concentration added from a burette
  • Titrand (Analyte): Solution of unknown concentration being analyzed
  • Equivalence point: Stoichiometric completion — reactants fully consumed
  • End point: Observable signal (color change, pH or current change) indicating equivalence has been reached
Advantages
  • Time-saving and labor-efficient
  • High precision
  • No need for reference standards
  • Relatively simple instrumentation
  • Applicable to kinetic measurements to establish reaction rates
Modern Developments
  • Spread of non-aqueous titration methods
  • Expanded application to very weak acids and bases
  • Potentiometric endpoint detection (improves precision)
  • Functional group analysis procedures
  • Integration with automated instrumentation
Pharmaceutical Applications
  • Captopril, albendazole, gabapentin — commercial dosage form analysis
  • Sparfloxacin — by non-aqueous titration
  • Estimation of degradation products of pharmaceuticals
  • Assay of bulk drug materials in compendial monographs

Chromatographic Techniques

Chromatography is based on differential affinity of analytes for a stationary phase vs. a mobile phase. Components with greater affinity for the stationary phase travel more slowly; those with lower affinity travel faster. This differential migration causes separation.
2a — Thin-Layer Chromatography (TLC)

Stationary phase: Adsorbent (e.g., silica gel) coated as a thin layer on glass, plastic, or aluminium support.

Mobile phase: Liquid solvent mixture that travels by capillary action.

Principle (adsorption): Compounds move over the stationary phase under the influence of the mobile phase. Compounds with greater affinity for the stationary phase travel slowly; others travel faster. Separated compounds appear as spots at different positions. Identity confirmed by:

Rf = distance traveled by compound ÷ distance traveled by solvent front

AdsorbentFormulaAdsorptive Power
AluminaAl₂O₃Most strong
CharcoalCMost strong
FlorisilMgO/SiO₂ (anhydrous)Most strong
Silica gelSiO₂Least strong

Advantages:

  • Minimal sample clean-up
  • Wide choice of mobile phases
  • High sample loading capacity
  • Low cost
  • Powerful tool for screening unknown materials in bulk drugs

Applications: Steroids, celecoxib, noscapine determination; impurity profiling and identification in early drug development.

Quantitative use: Spot elution followed by spectrophotometric measurement enables quantitative analysis.

2b — High-Performance TLC (HPTLC)

Same principle as TLC (adsorption; capillary mobile phase; affinity-based separation) but uses finer particle size adsorbents, automated sample application, and densitometric scanning for detection.

Key advantage: Multiple samples and standards are developed simultaneously but independently on each plate → increased reliability. The entire chromatogram is evaluated with a variety of parameters without time limits.

Advantages over TLC:

  • Fast separation with short analysis time
  • Simultaneous multi-sample analysis
  • Quantitative densitometric scanning
  • Increased reliability and reproducibility
  • Works well for complex or crude samples

Applications: Ethinyl estradiol, cyproterone, alfuzosin, tramadol, pentazocine quantitation.

2c — High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

HPLC is an advanced form of liquid chromatography used in separating complex mixtures of molecules in chemical and biological systems. It first appeared for bulk drug material assay in 1980 and has become the principal, most widely used method in therapeutic substance analysis.

Principle: Separation is based on the distribution of the analyte between a mobile phase (eluent) and a stationary phase (column packing material). Different chemical structures lead to different intermolecular interactions with the packing material → different "on-column" time → different retention times → separation.

System components in order:

  • Solvent reservoir — holds the mobile phase (eluent)
  • Pump — delivers eluent at high pressure, constant and pulseless flow (minimizes signal drift and noise)
  • Injection valve — introduces analyte into the eluent stream
  • Column — contains stationary phase where separation occurs
  • Detector — identifies and quantifies analytes after elution
  • Data processing unit — converts signals to chromatogram output
Detector TypePrincipleBest ForNotes
UV DetectorMeasures UV absorbance of analyte exiting columnUV-absorbing compounds (aromatics, conjugated systems)Can monitor several wavelengths concurrently via multiple wavelength scanning
PDA (Photodiode Array)Lined array of photodiodes senses a range of wavelengths simultaneouslyPeak purity analysis; method developmentOne injection detects all UV-absorbing compounds in the range. Can analyze peak purity by matching spectra within a peak. Applied in Iloperidone method development.

2d — Gas Chromatography (GC)

Gas chromatography is a powerful separation technique for volatile organic compounds, capable of detecting traces down to parts per trillion in some cases.

Principle: Sample is injected and enters a carrier gas stream (helium or nitrogen) which transports it into the separation column. Components are separated based on volatility and interaction with the stationary phase. The detector measures quantity of components that exit the column. A standard sample of known concentration is used for calibration — peak retention time and area of standard vs. test sample determine analyte concentration.

Main constraint: Comparative non-volatility of most drug substances means derivatization is virtually compulsory — chemical derivatization converts non-volatile drugs to volatile derivatives before analysis.

Additional limitation: High-molecular-mass products (polypeptides) or thermally unstable antibiotics cannot be analyzed by GC.

Applications:

  • Isotretinoin and cocaine — drug assay
  • Residual solvents in betamethasone valerate
  • Impurity analysis in pharmaceutical formulations
  • Gas liquid chromatography plays a substantial role in therapeutic substance analysis

Spectroscopic Techniques

3a — Spectrophotometry (UV/Vis)

Spectrophotometry is the quantitative measurement of the reflection or transmission properties of a material as a function of wavelength. Methods based on natural UV absorption and chemical reactions form an important group in pharmacopoeias.

Advantages: Low time and labour consumption; excellent precision.

Colorimetric methods are based on three principles:

  • Complex-formation reaction: Drug reacts with a reagent to form a colored complex
  • Oxidation-reduction process: Redox reaction produces a measurable color change
  • Catalytic effect: Drug catalyzes a color-producing reaction

Applications:

  • Blue tetrazolium assay — determination of corticosteroid drug formulations
  • Determination of cardiac glycosides
  • Routine assay of bulk materials in compendial monographs
3b — Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (NIRS)

NIRS is a rapid and non-destructive procedure that provides multi-component analysis of almost any matrix. It has gained wide appreciation in the pharmaceutical industry for raw material testing, product quality control, and process monitoring.

Principle: NIRS measures the absorption of electromagnetic radiation at wavelengths from 750 to 2500 nm. Absorptions correspond to overtones and combinations of vibrational modes involving C–H, O–H, and N–H chemical bonds. The resulting spectrum is unique to a sample — acting as a "fingerprint." Chemical and physical properties of organic molecules are encoded in this spectrum.

NIR spectrometer components:

  • Light source
  • Beam splitter system (wavelength selector)
  • Sample compartment
  • Optical detector
  • Data processing/analyzing system
Light SourcePropertiesIssues
QTH (Quartz-Tungsten-Halogen) lampsMost commonly used; low cost; high intensity; continuous spectral outputLow energy efficiency; heat generation; temperature and vibration sensitivity; long start-up times
LEDsHigher efficiency; lower power requirements; no excess heatCostly; not yet widely adopted in NIR spectrometers

Measurement modes:

  • Transmittance
  • Interactance
  • Transflectance
  • Diffuse transmittance
  • Diffuse reflectance (most commonly applied)

Choice depends on sample phase (solid/liquid), translucency, and particle size.

Advantages:

  • Easy sample preparation with no pre-treatments required
  • Fiber optic probes allow remote measurement
  • Chemical and physical parameters determined from a single spectrum
  • Non-destructive — sample can be recovered
3c — Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (NMR)

Since the first report in 1996 describing NMR for drug molecule screening, NMR-based analysis has grown rapidly. It is now applied in:

  • Quantitative analysis to determine drug impurities
  • Characterization of drug product composition
  • Quantification of drugs in pharmaceutical formulations and biological fluids

Principle: Many nuclei have spin and all nuclei are electrically charged. When an external magnetic field is applied, an energy transfer is possible between the base (ground) energy level and a higher energy level. This transfer takes place at a wavelength corresponding to radio frequencies. When the spin returns to its base level, energy is emitted at the same frequency. The signal is measured and processed to yield an NMR spectrum.

In practice: The sample is placed in a magnetic field. Nuclei are excited with radio waves into nuclear magnetic resonance, detected by sensitive radio receivers. The intramolecular magnetic field around each atom changes the resonance frequency (chemical shift) → gives access to electronic structure and functional group identity.

What NMR provides:

  • Definitive identification of monomolecular organic compounds
  • Detailed structure, dynamics, reaction state, and chemical environment information
  • Identification of chiral drugs, polymorphs, and impurities

Most common types: Proton NMR (¹H-NMR) and Carbon-13 NMR (¹³C-NMR). Applicable to any sample containing nuclei with spin.

3d — Fluorimetry & Phosphorimetry

Fluorescence spectrometry provides high sensitivity without loss of specificity or precision. Its application in quantitative analysis of drugs in dosage forms and biological fluids continues to grow.

Fluorescence principle:

  • A molecule absorbs a photon → enters an excited singlet state (S₁)
  • Upon relaxation, the molecule releases a photon — fluorescence emission
  • The emitted photon always has lower energy (longer wavelength) than the absorbed photon — the Stokes shift
  • Example: Fluorescein excited at ~500 nm (blue); emits at ~530 nm (green)
  • Each dye has an excitation spectrum and an emission spectrum, each with a characteristic peak

Phosphorescence:

  • Related to fluorescence — molecule absorbs radiation but does not immediately re-emit
  • Intersystem crossing from excited singlet state (S₁) to longer-lived triplet state (T₁) occurs
  • Delayed emission from T₁ → S₀ = phosphorescence
  • Both are forms of photoluminescence

The Jablonski diagram illustrates these transitions: Absorption → S₁/S₂ → Internal conversion/vibrational relaxation → Fluorescence (S₁→S₀) or Intersystem crossing → T₁ → Phosphorescence (T₁→S₀).

Electrochemical Methods

Overview & Principle

Electrochemical techniques deal with:

  • The study of chemical changes caused by the passage of an electrical current
  • The production of electrical energy by chemical reaction

These methods use a measurement of potential, charge, or current to determine an analyte's concentration or to characterize an analyte's reactivity. They are both qualitative and quantitative — based on electrochemical phenomena occurring within a medium or at phase boundaries.

Potentiometry

Measures potential difference between two electrodes. Used in pH meters, ion-selective electrodes, and as endpoint detection in potentiometric titrations.

Voltammetry

Measures current as a function of applied potential. Subtypes:

  • Cyclic voltammetry
  • Differential pulse voltammetry
  • Square-wave voltammetry

Example: clioquinol behavior studied using glassy carbon electrode over wide pH range.

Conductometry

Measures electrical conductivity of a solution. Used in precipitation and acid-base titrations for endpoint detection.

Colorimetry (Electrochemical)

Color-based electrochemical detection systems used in specific analytical setups alongside other electrochemical detection modes.

Dielectrometry

Measures dielectric properties of materials. Used for moisture determination and detection of non-conducting substances.

Kinetic Methods of Analysis

Kinetic methods have been developing since the 1950s and are undergoing major resurgence due to advancements in principles, automated instrumentation, data analysis methods, and analytical applications.
Principle

Kinetic methods use the rate of a chemical or physical process to determine an analyte's concentration. Particularly useful for reactions too slow for other analytical methods. For fast kinetics, automation allows sampling rates of more than 100 samples per hour.

Chemical Kinetic Methods

Use the rate of a chemical reaction and either integrated or differential rate law.

Integral method: Measure concentration at one or more time points after initiation → use integrated rate law.

Differential method: Measure reaction rate at time t → use differential rate law.

Important application: Quantitative analysis of enzymes and substrates, and characterization of enzyme catalysis.

Radiochemical Methods

Take advantage of decay of radioactive isotopes.

  • Direct measurement of decay rate determines isotope concentration
  • Neutron activation: Induces radioactivity in non-radioactive analytes
  • Isotope dilution: Radioactively-labeled analyte spiked into sample as internal standard
Flow Injection Analysis (FIA)

A liquid sample is injected into a moving, non-segmented, uninterrupted carrier stream. The injected sample forms a zone, transported to a detector that continuously records changes in absorbance, electrode potential, or other physical parameters.

NOT a separation technique — all components travel with the carrier stream. A second sample can be introduced before the first reaches the detector → ideal for rapid throughput.

4 Stages:

  • Injection
  • Dispersion
  • Detection
  • Washout

Electrophoretic Methods — Capillary Electrophoresis (CE)

Principle & Advantages

CE is based on the separation of charged analytes through a small capillary under the influence of an electric field. Solutes are perceived as peaks as they pass through the detector; peak area is proportional to concentration → allows quantitative estimation.

Advantages over HPLC:

  • More efficient separation
  • Faster analysis time
  • Only nanoliter injection volumes required
  • Takes place mostly under aqueous conditions
  • Smaller amount of sample and reagent consumed

Modes of CE:

  • Capillary Zone Electrophoresis (CZE): Simplest mode; separation based on charge-to-size ratio in buffer-filled capillary
  • Micellar Electrokinetic Chromatography (MEKC): Surfactant micelles act as pseudostationary phase; allows separation of neutral compounds
  • Isotachophoresis (ITP): Uses leading and terminating electrolyte system; good for preconcentration
  • Capillary Gel Electrophoresis (CGE): Gel-filled capillary separates by size; for nucleic acids and proteins
  • Isoelectric Focusing (IEF): Separation based on isoelectric point (pI) in a pH gradient; for protein characterization
  • Affinity CE: Uses specific biological interactions (antibody-antigen, receptor-ligand) for highly selective separations

Applied to pharmaceutical purity testing, bioanalysis of drugs, biopolymer analysis, and inorganic ion analysis.

7

Sequential Injection Analysis (SIA)

Overview

FIA was created by Stewart (USA) and Ruzicka & Hansen (Denmark) as the first generation of flow analysis automation. A second generation — Sequential Injection Analysis (SIA) — was developed by Ruzicka and Marshall in 1990.

Like FIA, SIA is a non-segmented continuous flow arrangement based on controlled dispersion. However, its mode of operation is based on programmable flow — using a single pump and multi-position selection valve.

Advantages over FIA:

  • Economy of reagents
  • Elevated sampling rates
  • Reduced waste generation
  • Greater automation flexibility

Pharmaceutical applications: Applied to solid matrices, pastes (ointments, creams), liquids (emulsions, suspensions, solutions) for determination of active ingredients in quality control of pharmaceutical formulations.

Hyphenated Techniques

Definition: The coupling of a separation technique with an on-line detection technique leads to the development of a hyphenated technique. The last two decades saw remarkable advancement in these techniques for pharmaceutical and therapeutic analysis.
Peer-reviewed reference
HPLC coupled with UV, fluorescence, or mass spectrometry has become the method of choice for bioanalytical method development, combining high separation efficiency with sensitive and selective detection across drug development stages. Journal of Analytical & Bioanalytical Techniques (2024)
LC-MS (Liquid Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry)

HPLC separation coupled with mass spectrometry detection. Provides structural identification and quantitation simultaneously. Method of choice for bioanalytical development.

Application: Meloxicam in biological samples and pharmaceutical formulations (HPLC-UV or MS/MS detection).

LC-ESI-MS (Electrospray Ionization)

Liquid chromatography with electrospray ionization–mass spectrometry. For qualitative and quantitative determination of drug metabolites.

Application: Metabolite analysis of Rhizome coptidis in rat urine; simultaneous determination of L-ascorbic acid and acetylsalicylic acid in aspirin effervescent tablets.

GC-MS (Gas Chromatography–Mass Spectrometry)

GC separation coupled with mass spectrometry. Provides definitive identification of volatile compounds.

Application: Identification of recreational drug (diphenyl-2-pyrrolidinemethanol) and its metabolites in rat urine.

LC-MS/MS (Tandem Mass Spectrometry)

Two stages of mass spectrometry — highest selectivity and sensitivity for complex matrices. Gold standard for pharmacokinetic studies.

Applications:

  • Drug-drug interaction studies (rabeprazole + clopidogrel)
  • Detection of carbapenemase activity from bacterial isolates
  • Determination of parabens in food
  • Pharmacokinetic profiling of CYP substrates (caffeine, warfarin, omeprazole, metoprolol, midazolam) in mini pigs
CE-MS

Capillary electrophoresis coupled with mass spectrometry. Combines the high separation efficiency of CE with the identification power of MS. Applied in analysis of pharmaceuticals.

Environmental Application of LC-MS/MS

Water samples from Langat River (Malaysia) extracted by solid phase extraction (SPE) and analyzed by LC-MS/MS → confirmed presence of mefenamic acid, salicylic acid, and glibenclamide in all river water samples, demonstrating the role of hyphenated techniques in environmental pharmaceutical surveillance.

Unit Three · Clinical Application

Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing — Disk Diffusion Method

Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing (AST) sits at the intersection of clinical microbiology, pharmacology, and public health. It provides clinicians with the data needed to choose the correct antibiotic for a given infection, while simultaneously generating the epidemiological intelligence that drives antibiotic policy and resistance surveillance. This unit covers the complete Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion method, the Stokes method, quality control procedures, and the major resistance mechanisms.

Definitions & Purpose

AST — Definition
The determination of the least amount of an antimicrobial chemotherapeutic agent that will inhibit the growth of a microorganism in vitro.
Purpose of AST
  • • Guide clinicians in antibiotic selection
  • • Accumulate epidemiological resistance data
  • • Formulate antibiotic policy
  • • Surveillance of community and hospital outbreaks
AST Methods

Disk Diffusion: Kirby-Bauer method, Stokes method

MIC: Broth dilution method, Agar dilution method

Early and accurate antimicrobial susceptibility results are crucial in sepsis treatment. Direct disk diffusion testing using positive blood culture broth provides results one day earlier than standard colony-based methods, demonstrating high predictive value and specificity for clinically important resistance phenotypes including MRSA and ESBL-producing Enterobacterales. Microorganisms, MDPI (2025)

Kirby-Bauer (Disk Diffusion) Method

Principle

Paper disks impregnated with an antimicrobial agent are placed on agar medium uniformly seeded with the test organism. A concentration gradient of the antibiotic forms by diffusion outward from the disk. Growth of the test organism is inhibited in the zone surrounding the disk where the antibiotic concentration exceeds the organism's MIC. The diameter of this zone of inhibition is measured and interpreted against CLSI breakpoints.

MHBA (5% Sheep Blood Agar)S. pneumoniae · Beta/alpha/non-haemolytic streptococciIncubate 37°C with 5% CO₂

Why MHA? MHA is preferred because: it shows acceptable batch-to-batch reproducibility; it is best for non-fastidious organisms; its low thymidine content prevents antagonism of sulphonamides and trimethoprim. Increased thymidine in the medium antagonizes the activity of sulphonamides.

Antibiotic Disks

Commercial Disk Handling

On receipt, check:

  • Manufacturing date
  • Expiry date
  • Lot number

Verify potency using ATCC strains. Storage:

  • Long-term: −20°C or −70°C
  • Routine use: 4°C–8°C
In-House (Paper Disk) Preparation

Antibiotic solution always prepared from pure substance. Stock concentrations depend on required disk strength.

Example — Ampicillin (needed: 2000 µg/ml for 10 µg disk):

  • 1 mg = 1000 µg
  • 2 mg = 2000 µg
  • 20 mg = 2000 µg/10 ml
  • 200 mg = 2000 µg/100 ml

Some antibiotics dissolved in organic solvent; others in sterile distilled water. Use only minimum volume of organic solvent to stabilize the antimicrobial powder.

1c

Inoculum Preparation

McFarland Turbidity Standard

McFarland Standard uses BaSO₄ — a barium sulfate turbidity standard that mimics known bacterial density.

0.5 McFarland = 2 × 10⁸ CFU/ml

Used for Gram-negative bacilli (GNB) and fast-growing organisms

1 McFarland

Used for Gram-positive cocci

Procedure:

  • Select 10 morphologically identical, well-isolated colonies
  • Inoculate in 1.5 ml Nutrient Broth
  • Incubate for 2 hours
  • Adjust opacity to McFarland standard (add broth to decrease turbidity, or more colony to increase)
1d

Inoculation & Disk Placement

Plate Size

6 antibiotics: use 85–90 mm Petri dishes · 2 antibiotics (QC): use smaller plates

Streaking

Streak plates within 15 minutes of opacity adjustment. Streak in 3 directions for uniform lawn of growth.

Disk Distances

Edge-to-edge between disks: ≥24 mm · Disk to plate periphery: ≥15 mm · Press disks firmly to agar surface — no air gap. Avoid overlapping zones of inhibition.

Reading & Interpretation

Susceptible (S)

Bacteria can be inhibited by the antibiotic at standard therapeutic doses. Organism is likely to respond to treatment.

Intermediate (I)

Bacteria can be inhibited only by higher doses. May respond if drug concentrates at the infection site (e.g., urinary tract).

Resistant (R)

Bacteria cannot be inhibited at achievable therapeutic concentrations. Drug not expected to be clinically effective.

Zone size measured in diameter (mm) using a transparent ruler. Interpreted for each organism/antibiotic pair by reference to CLSI guidelines. Only pure growth is considered. Inoculum must be adequate. No antibiotic disk misplacement.

QC Errors & Reading Problems

Larger Zone — Causes
  • Light inoculum (error in preparation)
  • Depth of medium too thin
  • MHA nutritionally unacceptable
Smaller Zone — Causes
  • Heavy inoculum (error in preparation)
One or More Zones Too Small/Large
  • Measurement error
  • Transcription error
  • Random defective disk
  • Disk not pressed firmly to agar surface
Reading Artifacts
  • Colonies within zone: Mixed culture or resistant mutants
  • Overlapping/indistinct zones: Disks too close or poorly streaked plate
  • Double zone: Proteus swarming (ignore) or fastidious organisms (e.g., beta Strep)
  • S. pneumoniae: Zone read by inhibition diameter — NOT by haemolysis pattern
  • One QC strain out of range: That one strain is the better indicator of the QC problem

Stokes Method

Principle & Interpretation

The Stokes method uses a control organism placed on the same plate as the test organism. Both are tested with the same antibiotic disk simultaneously. The zone size of the test strain is compared against the control strain zone size.

ResultCriterion
SusceptibleZone of test strain is ≥ zone of control strain
IntermediateZone of test strain is 2–3 mm smaller than control strain
ResistantZone of test strain is <2 mm smaller than control strain

Advantages of Disk Diffusion

Simplicity

Technically simple to perform. Does not require special equipment beyond a standard microbiology laboratory.

Economy

Reagents (disks, MHA) are inexpensive. Cost-effective for routine clinical laboratory use.

Clinical Communication

Results (S/I/R) are easily understood by clinicians and directly actionable in prescribing decisions.

Flexibility

Flexible regarding antibiotic selection — a wide range of disks can be used and customized to the institutional formulary.

Reproducibility

When CLSI guidelines and QC procedures are correctly followed, results are highly reproducible between laboratories.

Special Organisms & Resistance Mechanisms

5a — MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus)

Resistance mechanism:

  • mecA gene encodes Penicillin-Binding Protein 2a (PBP2a)
  • The active site of PBP2a does NOT bind methicillin or other beta-lactam antibiotics
  • PBP2a continues normal cell wall synthesis function → bacteria survive despite beta-lactam exposure
  • Acquisition of mecA confers resistance to ALL beta-lactam antibiotics — penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams

Clinical significance: Significant in both hospital-acquired (HA-MRSA) and community-associated (CA-MRSA) infections.

Drug of choice: Vancomycin & teicoplanin (injectable) · Rifampicin & linezolid (oral)

FeatureCefoxitin (Preferred)Oxacillin (Older)
RoleSurrogate marker — induces greater PBP2a expression in mecA-containing strains; also functions as test reagent to detect resistanceDirect test of beta-lactam resistance
StabilityStable drugDegrades on storage
Incubation16–18 hrs at 37°C24 hrs at 35°C
SupplementNo supplement necessary2–5% NaCl added to MHA
Zone readabilityClear, easily read zone with wider rangeNarrow range; harder to interpret

CLSI breakpoints (Cefoxitin for S. aureus): R = zone ≤21 mm | S = zone ≥22 mm

5b — Inducible Clindamycin Resistance (D-Zone Test)

Two different resistance mechanisms confer macrolide resistance in Staphylococci:

  • erm gene → methylation of 23S rRNA → resistance to erythromycin AND inducible or constitutive resistance to clindamycin
  • msrA gene → efflux pump → resistance to erythromycin ONLY (clindamycin remains susceptible)

The D-Zone test distinguishes between these mechanisms before reporting clindamycin susceptibility.

D-Zone Test Procedure:

  • Place erythromycin disk (15 µg) and clindamycin disk (2 µg) on MHA seeded with the organism
  • Edge-to-edge distance between disks: 15–26 mm
  • Incubate: 16–18 hrs at 37°C
  • Examine for flattening of the clindamycin zone on the side facing erythromycin (D-shape)
ResultObservationReport
D-Zone PositiveFlattening of clindamycin zone toward erythromycin disk (D-shaped zone)Clindamycin RESISTANT — erm gene present
D-Zone NegativeNo flattening — circular clindamycin zoneClindamycin SUSCEPTIBLE
Both ResistantBoth erythromycin and clindamycin zones absentClindamycin RESISTANT — constitutive resistance
The D-Zone test must be performed BEFORE reporting clindamycin as susceptible in any isolate resistant to erythromycin. Failure to do so could result in treatment failure.
5c — Vancomycin Resistance in S. aureus

Strains with reduced susceptibility to vancomycin are classified as:

  • VISA (Vancomycin-Intermediate S. aureus) or GISA — MIC value of 8 µg/ml; reduced susceptibility, not full resistance
  • VRSA (Vancomycin-Resistant S. aureus) — Full vancomycin resistance. Between 2002 and 2005, five different MRSA strains were detected for the first time with vancomycin resistance.

Detection methods:

  • Vancomycin disk diffusion (screening)
  • Vancomycin MIC — broth microdilution (gold standard)
  • Vancomycin agar screen: Brain Heart Infusion Agar (BHIA) with 6 µg/ml vancomycin — growth = possible resistance
5d — Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci (VRE)

Most common in E. faecium and E. faecalis. Six resistance gene types; VanA and VanB most commonly encountered.

Mechanism:

  • Alteration of terminal amino acid residues of the NAM/NAG-peptide subunits in cell wall precursor
  • D-alanyl-D-lactate variation results in loss of one hydrogen-bonding interaction
  • Loss of this single interaction causes approximately 1000-fold reduction in vancomycin affinity

VanA vs VanB:

  • VanA: High-level resistance to vancomycin AND teicoplanin (plasmid-mediated)
  • VanB: Variable vancomycin resistance; teicoplanin remains active

Detection: Disk diffusion · MIC by broth/agar dilution or E-test · BHIA with 6 µg/ml vancomycin screen plate

Alert reporting: VRE must be reported to HICC, MS office, and respective wards/units.

5e — High-Level Aminoglycoside Resistance (HLAR) in Enterococci

Enterococci are inherently resistant to low concentrations of aminoglycosides due to poor drug uptake by the enterococcal cell. This means aminoglycosides cannot be used as single agents for enterococcal infections.

However, low-concentration aminoglycosides combined with a cell wall-active agent (penicillin, ampicillin, vancomycin) achieve synergistic killing — the cell wall damage allows the aminoglycoside to enter and inhibit protein synthesis.

HLAR is usually the result of enzymatic inactivation of the drugs (aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes). When HLAR is present, the synergistic effect with cell wall agents is abolished.

Detection:

  • Disk diffusion: Gentamicin 120 µg disk
  • MIC: Gentamicin ≥ 500 µg/ml (HLAR threshold)
5f — Extended-Spectrum Beta-Lactamases (ESBL)

ESBLs are plasmid-mediated, diverse, complex, and rapidly evolving enzymes. They are the major mechanism of beta-lactam resistance in Gram-negative bacilli and pose a major therapeutic challenge.

Substrates hydrolyzed by ESBLs:

  • Third-generation cephalosporins: cefotaxime, ceftriaxone, ceftazidime
  • Monobactam: aztreonam

ESBLs do NOT efficiently hydrolyze cephamycins (cefoxitin) or carbapenems.

Organisms where CLSI guidelines apply: K. pneumoniae, K. oxytoca, E. coli, P. mirabilis

Co-resistance: ESBL producers exhibit co-resistance to aminoglycosides, quinolones, and SXT.

Clinical range: Infections from UTIs to life-threatening sepsis.

Beta-Lactamase ClassExamples
Restricted-spectrum (classical)TEM-1, TEM-2, SHV-1

ESBL Detection — Two-Step Process:

Step 1 — Phenotypic Screening:

  • Disk diffusion using extended-spectrum cephalosporins: cefotaxime (30 µg), ceftazidime (30 µg), ceftriaxone (30 µg), aztreonam (30 µg)
  • MIC dilution method (per CLSI screening thresholds)

Step 2 — CLSI Confirmatory Test (Combination Disk Test):

  • Disks used: Cefotaxime alone (CTX) vs Cefotaxime + Clavulanate (CTX+CL), and Ceftazidime alone vs Ceftazidime + Clavulanate
  • Positive ESBL: ≥ 3 two-fold (≥ 23) decrease in MIC of cefotaxime or ceftazidime when tested with clavulanate (4 µg/ml)
  • Also: Double Disc Approximation Test (DDAT) — distortion/expansion of zone toward clavulanate disk = ESBL positive

Drug of choice:

  • Carbapenems (imipenem, meropenem) — serious infections
  • Colistin, polymyxin, tigecycline — for carbapenem-resistant ESBL
  • Co-trimoxazole, nitrofurantoin, fosfomycin, gentamicin, amikacin, inhibitor combinations — for uncomplicated infections if susceptible
Peer-reviewed reference
Rapid automated disk diffusion can detect key resistance phenotypes including ESBL, carbapenemases, and MRSA within 6–8 hours by reading zone diameters at early time points — with meropenem susceptibility effectively separating Class A, B, and D carbapenemases. Journal of Antimicrobial Chemotherapy, Oxford Academic (2017)
5g — AmpC Beta-Lactamases

Chromosomal AmpC: Naturally produced by Enterobacter, Serratia, Citrobacter, Morganella, Providencia. Inducible — expression increased when exposed to certain beta-lactams.

Plasmid-mediated AmpC: Occur in E. coli and Klebsiella. Emergence predominantly in community-acquired infections.

Resistance profile:

  • Resistant to penicillins, cephalosporins including cephamycins (cefoxitin) — unlike ESBLs which do NOT hydrolyze cefoxitin
  • Resistant to beta-lactam + inhibitor combinations
  • Usually susceptible to carbapenems

Co-resistance: Aminoglycosides, SXT, quinolones.

5h — Carbapenemases

Carbapenems (imipenem, meropenem, ertapenem) represent the highest class of beta-lactam agents currently available. Carbapenemase production confers resistance to ALL beta-lactam antibiotics.
